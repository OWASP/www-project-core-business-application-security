# Code of Conduct & Project Rules for the OWASP Core Business Application Security Project (CBAS)

## 1. Purpose & Scope

The OWASP Core Business Application Security (CBAS) project aims to improve the security of enterprise business applications (e.g., ERP, CRM, HR, Finance, Cloud Business Platforms) through open research, tooling, documentation, and community collaboration.

This Code of Conduct applies to all CBAS project spaces, including but not limited to:  
- GitHub repositories  
- OWASP Wiki pages  
- Mailing lists, Slack/Discord channels  
- Meetings, workshops, hackathons  
- Conference talks and public demos under the CBAS name

## 2. Alignment with OWASP Global Code of Conduct

CBAS fully adopts and enforces the official:

OWASP Code of Conduct: https://owasp.org/www-policy/operational/code-of-conduct/

This document extends (not replaces) the OWASP Code of Conduct by adding CBAS-specific rules relevant to:  
- Security research  
- Business-critical applications  
- Vendor-neutral collaboration  
- Responsible disclosure  
- Conference and demo safety

In case of conflict, the OWASP Global Code of Conduct always takes precedence.  
- OWASP Conflict of Interest Policy  
https://policy.owasp.org/operational/conflict-of-interest  
- OWASP Project Policy  
https://policy.owasp.org/operational/projects  
- OWASP Conflict Resolution Policy  
https://policy.owasp.org/operational/conflict-resolution  
- OWASP Social Media Policy  
https://policy.owasp.org/operational/social-media

## 3. Expected Behavior

All participants are expected to:  
- Be respectful, inclusive, and professional  
- Assume good intent, especially in technical disagreements  
- Provide constructive feedback backed by facts and references  
- Respect different levels of expertise and backgrounds  
- Collaborate in the spirit of open security research  
- Credit others’ work appropriately

CBAS values technical rigor, curiosity, and collaboration over ego or vendor agendas.

## 4. Unacceptable Behavior

The following behaviors are examples of what’s not tolerated:

### Community Conduct 

- Harassment, discrimination, or personal attacks  
- Trolling, flame wars, or intimidation  
- Sexist, racist, or otherwise exclusionary language  
- Doxxing or sharing private information without consent

### Project Integrity

- Presenting unverified claims as facts  
- Deliberate misinformation or fear-mongering  
- Plagiarism or misrepresentation of authorship  
- Using CBAS platforms for personal or commercial attacks

### Vendor & Research Ethics

- Undisclosed vendor marketing or product pushing  
- Publishing exploits without responsible disclosure  
- Using CBAS work to for illegal and unethical purposes including but not limited to hacking,  defacing, doxing, or threatening organizations and individuals  
- Sharing customer-specific or confidential data

## 5. Vendor Neutrality & Transparency

CBAS is a vendor-neutral OWASP project.

All contributions, decisions, and public representations must prioritize technical merit, community value, and alignment with OWASP principles over commercial interests.

### Vendor Neutrality

- Vendor affiliation must be disclosed when relevant to a contribution or decision
- Technical contributions must stand on their own merit, independent of vendor origin
- Product mentions are permitted only when technically justified and clearly contextualized
- Sales pitches, lead generation, competitive marketing, or vendor bashing are not permitted

CBAS welcomes contributors from vendors, customers, consultancies, and academia, but not vendor-driven agendas that are aiming to promote brands, products, and services.

### Conflicts of Interest (COI)

CBAS does not define its own Conflict of Interest process.

All conflicts of interest are handled in accordance with the official OWASP Conflict of Interest Policy:
https://policy.owasp.org/operational/conflict-of-interest

Contributors and leaders are expected to disclose relevant conflicts of interest when participating in activities such as:

- Project governance or leadership decisions
- Selection, recommendation, or endorsement of tools, frameworks, or content
- Sponsorship-related discussions, visibility, or representation
- Project maintainer and project leaders must disclose any financial or personal interest (such as employment, consulting, ownership, or paid advocacy) that could reasonably be seen by an objective third party as:
- Favoring a particular vendor, product, or service in OWASP project decisions; or
- Allowing commercial benefit to that vendor or to themselves to improperly influence those decisions.

#### Disclosure Requirements

To ensure transparency and traceability, disclosures must be:
1. Public and Persistent
Disclosure must be made in a project location that is publicly visible and persistently documented (e.g., GitHub issue, pull request, repository README, or a dedicated disclosure section).
Ephemeral disclosures (e.g., private messages, live talks without written record, Discord-only statements) are not sufficient on their own.

2. Contextual
When relevant to a specific contribution (e.g., a PR involving a vendor-related tool), disclosure should be made directly within that contribution (e.g., via a PR template checkbox or statement).

3. Role-Based Disclosure
If a Project Leader or Maintainer has an ongoing relevant interest, this should be documented transparently in the repository (e.g., within the README or a governance section).

Individuals with a declared conflict of interest are expected to recuse themselves from related decision-making, in line with OWASP policy.

Unresolved or disputed conflict-of-interest matters may be escalated in accordance with the OWASP Conflict Resolution Policy:
https://policy.owasp.org/operational/conflict-resolution

## 6. Responsible Security Research & Disclosure

Because CBAS focuses on business-critical systems, all contributors must follow responsible research practices:

- No zero-day exploitation outside security lab environments and offensive security engagements approved by the owner of the asset  
- Use lab environments, intentionally vulnerable targets, or customer-approved scopes  
- Follow coordinated disclosure with affected vendors and system owners

Public releases should:  
- Focus on attack classes and patterns  
- Avoid weaponized, copy-paste exploitation  
- Emphasize defensive guidance and detection

## 7. Content & Contribution Rules
### Accepted Contributions

Accepted contributions are welcome when relevant to improve the security business application software security by supporting  security, and IT professionals, e.g. with:  
- Documentation, research papers, and whitepapers  
- Threat models, attack surface mappings  
- Detection ideas and SIEM use cases  
- Defensive tooling and scripts  
- Educational demos and labs

### Contribution Requirements

- Clear scope and assumptions  
- Reproducibility where possible  
- Proper citations and references  
- Open licensing compatible with OWASP (e.g., CC-BY, Apache 2.0)

### AI-Generated Content

- Must be reviewed, validated, and curated by a human  
- Raw AI dumps without validation may be rejected  
- Contributors remain responsible for correctness at the time of contribution

## 8. Conferences, Demos & Public Presentations

CBAS contributors must not present vendors, products, or commercial offerings as being endorsed, recommended, or officially supported by OWASP or the CBAS project.  

References to vendors or tools must be factual, neutral, and clearly contextualized, and must not include marketing language, comparative promotion, or calls to action.  

Repeated or severe brand-damaging behavior may be escalated under OWASP project governance and conflict-resolution policies.

When presenting CBAS-branded content:  
- Clearly mark demos vs real-world findings  
- Avoid naming real customers unless explicitly approved  
- Do not imply vendor endorsement by OWASP  
- Avoid sensationalism

CBAS content should educate, not embarrass.

## 9. Enforcement & Reporting

CBAS maintainers are responsible for addressing day-to-day conduct, moderation, and project-level issues in line with this Code of Conduct.

Project leaders and OWASP CBAS Leadership may:  
- Request changes or clarifications  
- Remove or moderate content  
- Restrict or suspend participation  
- Escalate issues in accordance with OWASP policies where required

Reports related to conduct or project issues may be raised with CBAS project leadership.  
Where issues cannot be resolved at project level, or where they involve policy, legal, financial, fiduciary, or reputational risk, they are escalated and handled in accordance with the OWASP Conflict Resolution Policy and other applicable OWASP operational policies.
https://policy.owasp.org/operational/conflict-resolution

OWASP’s policies define the applicable reporting channels, escalation paths, protections (including anti-retaliation), and decision authority for such cases.

Reports will be handled confidentially and respectfully.

### Removal of Participants

Project leadership may remove or restrict a participant’s involvement in the CBAS project if their behavior is abusive, disruptive, or otherwise inconsistent with this Code of Conduct or OWASP policies.

This may include, but is not limited to:
- Harassment, intimidation, or repeated hostile behavior
- Deliberate disruption of project work or discussions
- Repeated violations of this Code of Conduct
- Behavior that poses reputational, legal, or community risk to OWASP or CBAS

Such actions may include temporary or permanent removal from project spaces (e.g., repositories, communication channels, meetings).

#### Removal Process

Depending on the nature and severity of the issue, the following process may apply:

1. Initial Review and Opportunity to Respond
In cases that are not severe or urgent, the individual will be informed of the concerns and given an opportunity to provide a statement or clarification.
This may include a formal warning where appropriate.

2. Leadership Review and Decision
If the matter remains unresolved, or if no response is received within a reasonable timeframe (typically up to 30 days), CBAS leadership may decide on corrective action.
Decisions regarding removal or restriction require a majority vote of the CBAS leadership.

3. Severe Cases
In cases involving serious misconduct, legal risk, or immediate community harm, CBAS leadership may proceed directly to a decision without prior hearing, subject to a majority vote.

4. Conflict of Interest
Any CBAS leader who is the subject of a removal decision must recuse themselves from discussion and voting in accordance with OWASP Conflict of Interest principles.
Where appropriate, and especially in contested or high-impact cases, matters may be escalated and handled in accordance with OWASP’s Conflict Resolution and other applicable operational policies.

## 12. Roles & Responsibilities

The OWASP Core Business Application Security (CBAS) project follows a lightweight, merit-based governance model.  
Roles are defined to ensure clarity of responsibility, continuity of the project, and alignment with OWASP principles.

Holding a role is based on active contribution and stewardship, not titles.

### OWASP CBAS Leadership

**Role:** OWASP CBAS Leadership  
**Scope:** Overall project direction and OWASP alignment

Responsibilities include:  
- Providing overall strategic direction for the CBAS initiative  
- Ensuring alignment with OWASP mission, policies, and Code of Conduct  
- Representing CBAS within OWASP when required  
- Acting as an escalation point for governance or conduct issues  
- Supporting long-term sustainability of the project

This role focuses on coordination and oversight, not day-to-day project execution.

### Project Leader / Project Lead

**Role:** Project Leader (or Project Lead)  
**Scope:** Individual CBAS sub-projects, tools, or workstreams

Responsibilities include:  
- Leading and maintaining a specific CBAS project or sub-project  
- Acting as the primary “admin” and point of contact  
- Reviewing and approving contributions  
- Managing scope, roadmap, and quality  
- Ensuring responsible disclosure practices  
- Coordinating contributors and core contributors

Each CBAS project may have one or more Project Leaders, depending on scope and complexity.

### Core / Main Contributor

**Role:** Core Contributor (also referred to as Main Contributor)  
**Scope:** Active, trusted contributors within a project

Responsibilities include:  
- Contributing regularly and substantively to the project  
- Supporting the Project Leader with reviews, research, or maintenance  
- Helping onboard and guide new contributors  
- Acting as a “right hand” to the Project Lead when needed

Core Contributors are not project owners, but they play a key role in continuity and quality.

### Contributor / Project Member

**Role:** Contributor (or Project Member)  
**Scope:** Open participation

A Contributor is anyone who:  
- Submits content, code, documentation, or research  
- Participates constructively in discussions or reviews  
- Supports the project in a tangible way

This role is open and inclusive and does not imply long-term commitment or responsibility.

### Role Transitions

Contributors may become Core Contributors or Project Leaders based on sustained contribution and trust

Roles may evolve naturally over time

There is no formal promotion process; decisions are made by existing leadership based on merit and activity

### Decision-Making, Governance & Escalation

CBAS follows a pragmatic, consensus-driven governance model, aligned with OWASP project practices.  
Technical & Project Decisions  
- Technical decisions (e.g., scope, architecture, content inclusion) are made by the relevant Project Leader(s), informed by contributor discussion and consensus where possible.  
- When consensus cannot be reached, the Project Leader has final decision authority, acting in the best interest of the project and OWASP principles.  
- Significant or contentious decisions should be documented transparently (e.g., in issue trackers, pull requests, or meeting notes).

Code of Conduct & Moderation Decisions  
- Day-to-day moderation and conduct issues are handled by project leadership.  
- Contentious or unresolved conduct issues may be escalated according to the OWASP Conflict Resolution Policy.

Conflict of Interest (COI) Decisions  
- COI disclosure and recusal are handled in accordance with the OWASP Conflict of Interest Policy.  
- Decisions involving declared conflicts may not be resolved solely by the affected individual.  
- Unresolved or high-impact COI matters are escalated through OWASP governance channels.

Escalation Beyond the Project  
- Issues that cannot be resolved within CBAS, or that involve legal, fiduciary, policy, or reputational risk, are escalated to OWASP in accordance with applicable OWASP operational policies.

## 13. Activity & Role Maintenance

To keep the OWASP CBAS project active and sustainable, contributor and leadership roles require ongoing participation.

### Contributor Activity

Contributors are expected to demonstrate reasonable activity, such as:  
- Submitting or reviewing pull requests  
- Contributing research, documentation, or tooling  
- Participating in discussions, reviews, or meetings  
- Supporting project initiatives

If a contributor is inactive for 6 consecutive months, they may be:  
- Removed from the official contributor list  
- Moved to an `inactive contributor` or `alumni` status

This is not punitive and does not prevent future participation.

### Project Leadership Activity

Project Leaders and Maintainers are expected to:  
- Actively guide contributions  
- Support project direction and quality  
- Participate in governance and security disclosure decisions

If a Project Leader or Maintainer is inactive for 6 consecutive months, they may be:  
- Removed from the leadership role  
- Transitioned to contributor or alumni status

Leadership roles are based on active stewardship, not titles.

### Reinstatement

Former contributors or leaders may request reinstatement at any time.

Reinstatement is not automatic and is based on demonstrated renewed engagement and commitment to the project.

The following applies:  
1. Request and Initial Review  
A reinstatement request must be submitted to CBAS leadership.  
For project-specific roles, the relevant Project Leader must be involved in the review.  
2. Trial / Grace Period  
Reinstatement may be granted subject to a three-month trial period, during which renewed and sustained contribution is expected.  
Failure to demonstrate consistent activity during this period may result in the reinstatement being revoked.  
3. Approval Process  
Reinstatement of Core Contributors or Project Leaders requires a majority vote of the CBAS leadership.  
For sub-project roles, the relevant Project Leader’s agreement is required in addition to the leadership vote.  
4. Decision Timeline  
CBAS leadership aims to decide on reinstatement requests within one month of submission, subject to availability and discussion requirements.

There is no formal re-application process, but reinstatement decisions are based on sustained engagement, project needs, and alignment with this Code of Conduct.

### Transparency & Exceptions {#transparency-&-exceptions}

- Role changes due to inactivity should be communicated transparently  
- The affected individuals will be notified in advance via a know communication path  
- Temporary inactivity (e.g., parental leave, health, job changes) can be communicated to maintainers and will be respected
